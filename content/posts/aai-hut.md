At the end of January 2026 I took a [3-day AAI Avalanche Level 1 course](https://coloradomountainschool.com/course/avalanche-level-1-hut-trip/) through Colorado Mountain School. The course is offered in a hybrid capacity but I opted for one the hut-versions which take place at several 10th Mountain Division huts. Living in Denver, I knew the front range would be my closest and primary ski range, so the Broome Hut made the most sense.

Meeting up at the Berthoud Pass warming hut, our group were the only cars in the lot. Yes we were early, and yes it was a week day, but 2026 was also not the season for snow; a recent storm had given the area barely 2" of fresh snow (just enough to hide the rocks). I wasn't totally unfamiliar withe area. Near the end of 2025 I had acquired some ~old~ well-loved Dynafit Huascaran fit with Marker F10 frame bindings. At the time I knew I wanted to get into backcoutry skiing, but I also knew that there were enough horror stories that it wasn't worth trying to hit big stuff until I had the proper training. I had done some practice runs though Loveland's (limited) uphill access, but it was really just to get a feel for the gear. I knew of Berthoud because it was named as a popular backountry spot close to Denver, and it the famous [Light Tours of Colorado](https://beaconguidebooks.com/product/light-tours-of-colorado-atlas/) by Lou Dawson includes 2 different zones with low avalanche danger. One of those zones happened to be directly out of the parking lot our group met up in.

![](/images/broome-hut.jpg "The final accent to the hut")

A small group, 9-ish, was made even smaller by 2 out-of-staters not showing up (one family emergency, one with with luggage lost at DFW who showed up a day later). We had two seasoned instrcutors who took turns leading. After intros in the waming hut we got back in cars and continued down the road to the Second Creek trail head where we booted up and started our decent. Skinning uphill is one thing, but skinning uphill with 3 days worth of food and clothes was another - we took plenty of breaks on the accent and I was happy to not be the last one up.

The hut itself is phenominal. As luck would have it, one of the more proactive volunteers was a student in the course, and she happily gave everyone a tour of the facilities (the coolest of which is the massive composting toilets seen in the basement). Toilets aside, the hut featured 4 bedrooms packed with bunk beds, a very large kitchen with 8+ propane burners and a comedically large pump-spiget for water, a large open social area with a modern pellet stove, and probably some of the best views one get get while cozied up by a fire (see the image at the top of this post). 

![](/images/suface-hoar.jpg "Surface hoar, a type of snow consiting of beautiful delicate feathers. These feathers can sometimes be buried, causing a weak layer under the surface")

The course covered several aspects of avalanche safety. Noteably, all the factors that influence an avalanche occuring, including snow conditions, weather conditions, the terrain, the aspect, the elevation, etc. They dicussed the different types of avalanche problems (there are a lot), the problems we deal with most in Colorado (Persistent Slab), and the different indicators that an avalanche may trigger (in the case of a persistent slab, there are no good visual indicators...).

When describing the course content to friends, I found myself comparing it to high school sex ed: The class is front-loaded with information about the dangers of avalanches, how the occur, and how to avoid terrain that may carry of a risk of avalanching. You can think of this as the promotion of absitnence - the best way to avoid an avalanche is not to ski in avalanche terrain!! But... in case you do ...

A major portion of the course is identifying what risks you're willing to make for an objective. Another major portion is the basics of avalanche rescue. Anyone who has read up on backcountry safety will know this common trifecta of safety gear: beacon, shovel, probe. These 3 pieces of gear are the primary tools used for identifying a buried victim and digging them out. On the note of risk taking, a useful piece of perspective came from one of the instructors recounting his mentor's advice. The history is that the beacon/shovel/probe technique we know today didn't always exist and that, for a long time, people were mostly just raw-dogging it. The mentor's advice was this: when identifying a line that you would like to ski, ask yourself the question "If I didn't have my beacon/shovel/probe, would I still feel safe skiing this line?". That piece of advice really stuck with me - the idea that the safety gear we carry can sometimes give us the confidence to take on additonal risk. Obviously within my lifetime I would like to ski some big risky terrain, but the hope is that I'll never have to use the safety gear that I carry.

![](/images/snow-pit.jpg "Digging a snow pit is fun work!")

Following a day of instruction, we would put on our skis and have some fun in the snow. Examples include quick-drawing probes and shovels (this helped me realize that I need more modern gear), pair-excersises hunting down buried beacons, practice reading a digital map for navigation and identifying hazards, and digging a snow a pit. I was both sad but relieved to find out that the average person is not digging snow pits. They look pretty cool whenever the come accross my social media page, but the rules and tests that go into identifying snow hardness and release feel arbitrary. The result of digging a pit is that you can see with your own eyes the history of the winter season: every storm, freeze/thaw, windy cold spell is archived in layers of snow. But the reality is that the great people at the [CAIC](https://avalanche.state.co.us/) have already dug the pits and identified those layers for you, and they've hand-written the results into daily forecasts for you to read in the comfort of your own home.

My final takeaway from the course is a feeling of fear and inspiration. On one end, there are sooooo many factors and risks. Learning that Colorado's main problem type is persistent slab, a problem type with no good indicators and one that can be easily triggered reomtely, is scary and it makes me hesitant of any terrain (even terrain that is safe!). On the other hand, learning that layers repair themselves over time and that by spring, assuming CAIC marks a given zone as green, the corn snow will be minde for that taking - that's inspiring. It's obvious why everyone says to take this course before venturing into the backcountry. It's also obvious why every season experienced people die in the mountains.

Here's to playing it safe, and hopefully getting some good turns in come Spring!
