Diagnosing That I Do NOT Have a Blown Head Gasket

2026/05/03

Tags: Car Baja

I am the proud owner of a 2003 Subaru Baja, but the thing is low-key a POS. When I bought the car in September of 2025 I had a vague idea of what I was getting into. The main issue was a P420 code which led to my first home-repair: replacing the catalytic converter. Around that time it got an oil change, and a top-off of coolant. Much time has passed since then, and honestly no major issues. But the other week I noticed that my coolant is once again at low, and that’s never a good thing…

The big scare with missing coolant is an issue with the car’s head gasket. Essentially there is a thin rubber gasket that sits between the main engine body and the engine “head”. The head is the top part of the engine that defines the combustion chamber; this part of the engine is where your spark plugs are igniting tiny gasoline explosions which ultimately make the car move. As one might expect, there is a lot of heat and stress around this area of the engine and so, to help mitigate that heat, coolant is pumped through the engine block around the heads to cool the engine. And thus a problem can start to form. If the head gasket fails, coolant that would otherwise be adjacent to the moving components can start to seep into the combustion chambers. The coolant can start to mix with the oil, the oil can start to mix with the coolant, exhaust gases can start to escape through the seems, the engine can start to overheat, and all because a $25 piece of rubber and metal got bent out of shape. Repairing a blown head gasket means disassembling a portion of the engine which is not something I am comfortable yet, but I do find inspiration in the people that are. Paying a professional will put you out $1,000+ .

So here are the steps I took to try and diagnose my issue:

Checking if the Previous Owner Lied

The Marketplace listing claimed the head gasket had already been replaced, which in theory should reduce the probability of me having the same issue. This can be checked relatively quickly by looking for the metal edges of the gasket poking out from the engine. Older head gaskets (like the factory one when the car was made) are a single sheet of metal. The newer gaskets are a sandwich design with multiple layers. This video shows off that older, single-layer, design. On my Baja I could see multiple layers, suggesting that the gasket was replaced at some point with the newer design.

Checking the Oil Cap

A gasket failure could allow coolant to seep into the oil pathways that are also in the engine head. Coolant can make it’s way back to oil pan where it can mix, boil, evaporate, and do other nasty chemical nonsense. A “milky” oil cap can indicate that something is wrong:

In my case the oil cap was as clean as a whistle.

Checking the Oil

I didn’t do a full check of the oil, but I had done an oil change a couple months back. Milky oil coming out of the pan is another visual indicator that bad things are mixing. Color is described as chocolate milk. Here is a photo from my oil change (I was tilting into the light to try and show the sparkles) and a confirmed case of oil and coolant mixing. Mine looks a little brown, but honestly not as bad as other photos I was seeing online; I think it passes.

Checking the Exhaust

If coolant is burning up in the combustion chamber then it can also be detected by checking the exhaust. Nitrogen, CO2, and H2O are the main exhaust byproducts, but the boiling of the coolant will cause excess water vapor and other tasty chemicals to enter the exhaust system. The way this manifests is through consistent white smoke coming out of the tail pipe. You can also feel the end of the tail pipe after a driving session to see if it’s wet - a wet tail pipe indicates excess water vapor which probably means coolant getting burned in the engine. I get a little white exhaust when I fist start the car, but it goes away after driving a bit and the tail pipe is as dry a sober desert.

Checking the Radiator

Inversely, a head gasket issue may cause exhaust gases to escape through the coolant pipes and into the radiator. A quick test is to remove the radiator cap (when cold), start the car, and see if exhaust bubbles start forming at the top. This was not the case for my car.

Conclusion

So that’s why a don’t have a blown head gasket. Probably. This post was really one big long cope to convince myself that everything is fine. Thanks for reading, and wish me luck in finding the actual source of my coolant leak.
